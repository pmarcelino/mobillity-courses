You’ve made the jump from learner to operator. The notebook you opened is the same kind of notebook working analysts keep open during the day — and you now have the workflow to fill it with real code that does real work.

What You’ve Accomplished

Look back at what was abstract a module ago and is concrete now.

  • You’ve named the workflow. Direct the model in plain English, it writes Python, you run it, you read the result, you ask the next question. You can explain why this loop is more trustworthy than asking for an answer in prose.
  • You’ve loaded real data with an LLM. In Google Colab, directing Claude, you took a CSV from file-on-disk to a table you can ask questions about — using all three interaction modes: asking for code, asking for explanations, and pasting errors back.
  • You’ve inspected a dataset with pandas. You know what .shape, .info(), .describe(), .isnull().sum(), and .value_counts() each tell you, and you know that spotting a negative trip duration or a column of strings that should be numbers is the analyst’s job.
  • You’ve learned which formats to trust. Why xlsx hides structure that does not survive conversion, why PDF tables garble in extraction, why screenshots lose numeric precision — and why CSV and GeoJSON pass through an LLM intact.

Real-World Impact

You can now sit in front of a GTFS export or a citizen-survey CSV and make the first real move — load it, inspect it, direct the LLM at the right questions, and catch a bad format choice before it becomes a wasted afternoon. That is the day-one move of an analyst working with mobility data.

Key insight

The LLM writes the code; you decide what to ask and how to read what comes back. That ownership is what turns generated output into a defensible answer.
