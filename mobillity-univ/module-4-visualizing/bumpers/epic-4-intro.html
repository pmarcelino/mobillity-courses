Welcome to the Module

You have cleaned and explored real mobility data, and you have found things worth showing. This module is about showing them — clearly. You will not learn plotting syntax. You will learn to direct the LLM to generate the chart, read what it produces, and refine it in plain language until it communicates.

That distinction is the whole module: the first chart is never the final one. The skill is not drawing — it is choosing the right view and improving it through conversation.

What You’ll Learn

Across six short lectures, you will move from a single bar chart to an interactive map of a city’s network — all directed, never hand-coded.

  • Choose the right chart for the question. Match a question’s shape — comparison, trend, distribution, relationship, geography — to a chart family, and use the LLM to prototype two or three options and pick the one that reads clearest.
  • Generate static and statistical charts. Direct the LLM to build publication-ready matplotlib charts, and reach for seaborn when you need to see a distribution or correlation that a bar of averages would hide.
  • Build interactive and geographic views. Add hover, zoom, and filtering with plotly, and put mobility data where it belongs — on a map — to reveal coverage, clustering, and gaps that no table can show.
  • Iterate to a polished result. Run the full loop — generate, see, describe the gap, regenerate — and learn when a chart needs another round and when it needs a fresh start.

Why Visualizing Mobility Data Matters

Transport is spatial and temporal by nature: where stations cluster, when usage peaks, how service spreads across a city. These patterns are invisible in a spreadsheet and obvious on the right chart. For a mobility analyst, the map and the time-series are not decoration — they are the finding.

Remember

The first output is a draft. You improve it by describing the gap between what you see and what you want — in words — and the LLM rewrites the code. Three or four rounds to a polished chart is normal, not a sign you did something wrong.

Ready to Begin?

The module starts before any chart is drawn — with the question every visualization should answer first: of all the ways to show this, which one makes it clearest?
