What You’ve Accomplished

Five lectures, one repeatable pipeline from a verified result to a stakeholder-ready deck.

  • You structure an analysis as a story. You arrange findings as Minto’s Pyramid suggests — main point first, supporting reasons next, evidence last — and use the one-sentence test to keep the message decision-ready.
  • You adapt one message for different audiences. You direct the LLM to reshape one analysis into several communications by naming who is reading, what they must decide, and how much detail they expect.
  • You generate slide decks from your notebook. You have the LLM write the code that builds the presentation — a PowerPoint file, an interactive web deck, markdown slides, or academic pages — instead of building it by hand.
  • You run the whole pipeline end-to-end. You structure the narrative, draft the brief, generate the slides, review the result, and refine over quick rounds — the notebook your single source of truth.

Real-World Impact

The analysis that gets acted on is rarely the most sophisticated — it is the one its audience understood. Taking a result from a raw dataset to a presentation a transport authority can decide on is what turns your work from an exercise into influence.

Key insight

The path from analysis to impact has two equal halves: doing the analysis right, and telling its story in the way your audience can act on. You can now do both.

What’s Next: Completing the Course

This was the final module. You have travelled the whole arc — clean, explore, visualise, verify, and now communicate — and can run it end-to-end with the LLM at every step. What is left is to put it to work on an analysis of your own.

Before You Continue

Make sure the core moves feel solid:

  • Can you state what your analysis proves in one sentence, then name the two or three reasons that make it believable?
  • Can you see why the same finding should sound different for a director deciding a budget than for the riders who feel it on their commute?
  • Can you direct the LLM to write code that turns your narrative and charts into a deck — and review what it produced?
  • Do you know when to reach for a PowerPoint file, an interactive web deck, quick markdown slides, or formal academic pages?
