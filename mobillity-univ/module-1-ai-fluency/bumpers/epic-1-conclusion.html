What You’ve Accomplished

This module was about calibration. Look back at how much your sense of what the model is doing right now has sharpened.

  • You’ve explained how LLMs generate text. You can describe next-token prediction in plain language and recognize when a platform is calling a tool instead of predicting.
  • You’ve measured data in tokens. You understand what counts as a token, what the context window holds, and you can estimate whether a given mobility dataset has any chance of fitting.
  • You’ve named the quality limits inside the window. You can describe lost-in-the-middle, conversation-level drift, and the prediction roots of hallucination.
  • You’ve watched casual prompting break down. You saw the constraints compound in one realistic Barcelona analysis and understand why the rest of the course teaches the LLM to write code instead.

Real-World Impact

The instinct you’ve built is the difference between a student who is “good with ChatGPT” and an analyst who can be trusted with a real dataset. When a stakeholder hands you a GTFS feed or a citizen-feedback corpus, you now ask the right questions before you ask the LLM anything — the reflexes that separate confident output from defensible output.

Key insight

Fluency is not accuracy. Every LLM output is a prediction. The professional move is to give the model a job it can do reliably — write code — rather than ask it to do the analysis itself.
